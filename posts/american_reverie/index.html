    american reverie

    Tags: drafts

    I recently read an article by Patrick Wyman on how empires in decline have their wars reverberate back on them. The gist of it is this: empires typically have their domestic affairs shielded away from their foreign actions. It’s only when empires are in decline that you see the actions of empires in foreign lands affect their domestic affairs. In the case of the US, we see the failed wars of Iraq and Afghanistan contribute to the militarization of the police and erosion of the rights of domestic citizens, such as the case of Portland and federal paramilitary snatching up protestors.

    I agree on multiple aspects, since 2001, under the arching theme of “never again”, American domestic institutions have gradually acquired more and more tools of action and defense themselves, becoming what Bruno Macaes calls a “citadel on a hill”. However, I want to push Wyman’s idea further: recent events, such as the paramilitaries in Portland, the alt-right militias, and even the protests can be traced back to America’s foundational culture, the concept of settler colonialism in the frontier.

    Settler colonialism isn’t like the typical British/India colonialism, where you have one ruling group (i.e. the British) dominating another group (the Indians). Rather, settler colonialism is about replacing the indigenous population, and taking over their lands. This is deeply rooted in American society, it’s the foundational reason why certain groups of Americans perceive themselves as “real” Americans. These people largely buy into the concept of a “coastal elite”, typically hold blue-collar jobs, and likely live in the Midwest or at least majority racially/culturally homogenous areas. While there are many people who still buy into the concept of “real” Americans even when they defy these qualities, the point is this: there is a group of Americans who perceive themselves as a more legitimate heir to American society than others.

    The ideology from this entire group of Americans can be traced back to settler colonialism. Settler colonialism in America allowed the expanding western frontier to buy into this idea of a “blank slate”, where one can manifest their destiny. The “West” was blank, a boundless resource to be exploited. This conveniently sets aside the brutality inflicted on the Native Americans, dispossessing of their land either through arms or by law.

    In conjuction to the the concept of a frontier, the American south exploited its resources in another: the insitution of slavery. Slavery, at it’s core, disposesses people of the fruits of their labor. Slavery takes away the results of what you do in exchange for nothing. The question then becomes: why did the US never enslave Native Americans? The colonalization of the midwest began in the late 1820’s, slavery was not formally abolished until 1865. Why was there no institution setup to enslave native americans, who would’ve been far cheaper than importing slaves?

    The answer is that black slaves had no collective claims to the land they worked on. Black slaves, stripped of their heritage and past, could not have any claim that they had settled on the land they worked on, while native americans did. These are the twin engines of American settler colonialism, taking away land from the native americans, taking away the products of labor from black slaves. David Ricardo formulated the idea of land and its relationship to economics, Marx linked the idea of labor and economics. American settler colonialism, with its economic focus linked these ideas with its two subservient populations: Ricardo for the native americans, Marx for the black slaves.

    It is in this bubble that you can coherently form the concept of a “real” American. The frontiersman of westward expansion were only able to expand out onto the pristine plains by driving out the native populations, fueled with tools provided through the economic engine of slavery. The frontiersman held up an image of “self-made”, man against nature, even though the conditions that allowed for the frontiersman were created through immense suffering of the native americans and african americans.

    American society of the post-World War II era was formed in much of the same way. In the era of the American empire expanding overseas after World War II, Americans found themselves in the same situation, they could claim to live in a meritocracy where all you had to do was work hard, even though this was based on the suffering of people all over the world. The twin engines that powered America’s economy, native americans and black slaves, had been replaced with people who buy American goods overseas. In the wake of the Cold War, America’s manufacturing and exporting economy required a healthy consumer base, and the only way to corner the global market was to ensure that America was the dominant power and exporter. Thus the Cold War began, and the propping up of brutal dictators to fight against “communism”. Native peoples in South Vietnam, Chile, and the other countries where the US fought proxy wars or supported brutal regimes did not die for “freedom” or “halting of communism”, they died so that the US could maintain countries to export to. Ultimately, Americans in the Cold War could tell themselves American society was fair and equal, even as the conditions for a “fair and equal” American society was on the backs of millions overseas.

    Back to modern day America. Modern day America is reeling from its failures in Iraq and Afghanistan, yet why did the same thing not happen in the wake of the Vietnam war? How did America disengage from Vietnam with, as Goerge Kennan phrased it, “total withdrawal, followed by silence and detachment”1?

    The answer is television, and by extension, social media. The first war that was truly televised, and made-for-tv was the first Gulf War. Broadcasted on television, the US’s quick destruction of Iraqi forces in Kuwait shocked the world of American power. Technical superiority, there for all to see. This led to a revival in perceptions of American strength, no longer was America the country that lost a war to South Asian farmers, it was the country with vast technical support, and ready to show everyone. The fall of the Soviet Union, only 10 months after the end of the first Gulf War, made American society seem all the more indomitable.

    It is this reviere that Iraq and Afghanistan shake us out of. No matter the technical superiority, the US military was unable to establish a functioning democracy in Iraq, and barely able to rebuild society in Afghanistan. After nearly two decades of war, the American spell has thoroughly been broken, its failures laid bare for every citizen to see, share, like and subscribe.

    This is how “imperial wars come home”, as Wyman calls it. Imperial wars do not come home by only bringing their guns and training back back2, they come back by shaking us out of our fugue states. The failures of Iraq and Afghanistan prompt Americans to do real soul-searching of their society, and what they now see is a society of rising inequality, leaders asleep at the wheel, and unresolved racism. American society is deeply adept at amnesia, no true transitional justice has ever been held for native americans, and the racial wealth gap between blacks and whites in the US should be self-explanatory. This leads to the rise of “bro” culture, a culture where one can escape to a world that seems more meritocratic than the America of today. It leads to the alt-right militias, who are far more devoted to their anti-authority ideology than their racism. The alt-right militias fight for an America of the past, an America whose sins can be glossed over, atrocities that can be followed by “silence and detachment”. Imperial wars come home by bringing more than the toys of war, imperial wars have come by exposing the wounds in American society.
