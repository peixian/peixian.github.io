    nationalism and outsiders in the middle east - thesis

    Introduction

    Works relating to constructivist approaches to nationalism has left much to be desired with regards to the contemporary role of religion. Using Hinnebusch’s three tiered model of nationalism, comprising of super-state, state-level, and sub-state actors, I propose that analysis of religion confined to a single stratum does not fully explain the power and integration of religion.

    Benard Anderson’s seminal work on imagined communities fails to account for the contemporary status of religion. While Anderson provides for religion as a singular component of a nation or a ethnicity, the relationship between religion and nationalism is strictly one way. For Anderson, nationalism has not supplanted or superceded religion anderson_imagined_2006, but rather religion exists as a source for nationalism. Nationalism can harness religion as a place of gestation, or it can utilize religion to spread its beliefs. However, both of these roles holds religion as an unspeaking entity, either something unchanging from the past that can be pointed to as a crux, or something that can be molded. Religion, for Anderson, is a tool, much like ethnicity or language.

    Other works attempt to tackle religion confined within a single stratum, typically only on the sub-state or supra-state level. Works such as saunders_ummah_2008 in the wake of the ‘Cartoons Affair’ that generated significant backlash across a multitude of nations against depicitions of the Prophet Muhammad towards a Dutch newspaper typically analyze religion as a solely supra-state level issue. Religion here is interesting becuase it influences the digital field, and theories can be drawn about how such online communities can potentially constitute a “nation”. However, this again reduces religion to a single shared attribute. Westphalian ideas of nations, such as clearly delineated zones of soverignty, equality of citizens, are applied to the digital realm via the linkage through religion. Here, religion is not far removed from ethnicity, an “Islamic” nation in the digital realm is not far removed from a “Asian” nation. Notions of “pan-Islamism” fall along these lines as well, placing religion jostling for position against nationalism. What this type of analysis lacks is an explanation for the role of religion on the sub-state level, the implict assumption is that a supra-state “nationalism” encompasses state level and sub-state level nationalisms.

    The work of Miroslav Hroch attempts to diagnose religion as part of a state, although on a purely mechanical level. In the wake of the fall of the Ottoman empire, a multitude of Balkan nations began a process to nationalize and control the levers of religion. Retooling religion as a myth and symbol of the state, Balkan nations seized control of the clergy and began to promulgate ideas that the church “had served as a preserver of the nation through the dark centuries under the yoke” anscombe_state_2014, 146. Here, the interpretation of religion is strictly state level, Orthodox christianity could be partitioned among the various states and its pieces seized upon as a symbol and a tool of legimtacy for the new governments. The outlier was Albania, which had completely banned religion in the 1960’s. Drawing religion into the political sphere on the national level would later branch into religion as an opposition channel to nationalism as well as in conjunction with nationalism anscombe_state_2014, 221. In addition, religion and the state can be defined negatively. The sheer existence of a heterogenous mix of religions within a single nation can also circumscribe the parameters of nationalism, forcing nations to create national myths arond it, causing nations to engage in writing history that emphasises the comsopolitian and diverse details.

    Discussion on Current Literature

    The analysis of religion, whether contrary or compatible with nationalism in the Balkans also suffers from the same types of issues as analyzing religion on the supra-state level. It fails to consider religion as a fluid, dynamic force that can move between and simultanously occupy all three tiers at once. In this respect, I propose extending Hinnebusch’s three tier model by creating the same model, with three different stratum for religion. Just as the Hinnebusch’s model requires the definition of a nation to create supra-national and sub-national identities, defining religion allows for the a model containing supra-religious and sub-religious communities. This type of thinking is not new, Klaus-Jürgen Nagel and Ferran Requejo have proposed treating nationalism and religion as analogous pheonomena requejo_coll_politics_2015.

    To interrogate this model, clear definitions must be drawn out for the three strata of religious, supra-religious, and sub-religious. In order to prevent treating religion as a source or explanation for nationalism requejo_coll_politics_2015, I will define religion here as a strict communal ideaology, shared among a group of people, adhereing to broadly similar specific customs, but also containing significantly different sects (TODO: this line is very bad, need to revise it to sound better). By this definition, Islam and Christianity are considered on the religious level, while Twelver Shi’ism, Hannafi Sunnism, and Orthodox Christianity are considered sub-religious. It is then clear that sectarnian identities within a single religion are considered sub-religious, while consociation between different religions is along the religous strata. This places Lebanon’s and Iraq’s cosociation systems as both religious and sub-religious, silmutaenously managing differences between different religious and between single religious. This also allows for space to explain why Shi’a and Sunni political parties do not always align with each other in the Lebanese parliment against Christian communities, the clashes between the Future Movement (majority Sunni) and Hezbollah/Amal (both Shiite) can be explainined because the Ta’if system locks parties against each other on the sub-religious level (TODO: find actual sources for amal/hez voting records, this was mentioned in the lebanese politics podcast #37).

    While the definition of religious and sub-religious are relatively clear cut, what is supra-religious? Supra-religious identities have signficant overlap with supra-national identities, such as pan-Arabism or even international institutions. This allows for the linkage and understanding of the both models, nationalism can be interpreted both along the lines of a single nation, and alson along the lines of a single religion. This understanding provides space for the types of understanding of the online religious communities, such as the Muslim global community saunders_ummah_2008, as they are supra-national but simultaneously religious.

    No model is complete, however, without being able to define it negatively. The religious strata and its linkages to the national model can be defined negatively via the notion of jihad. I propose that jihad represents the simultanous breakdown of both strata, when the middle nation level is absent from the nation model and when religion is absent from the religious model. It is also important to note that while jihad has broadly been painted as an Islamic affair, different sects and schools of religious jurisprudence have singificantly different interpretations of jihad. The interpretation of a Hanbali Sunni on jihad is quite far from the interpretations of an Ismaili Shi’a. Due to this, we must place jihad on the sub-religious strata.

    This concept allows us to see that jihad is defined negatively through the lack of a strong nation, and a strong broad religious community. In this framework, the Islamic State in Iraq and the Levant are defined by the empowerment on the supra-nation and sub-religious strata. In Bosnia, the jihad against the Serbs was the opposite, as it was defined by the empowerment on supra-religious and sub-nation strata.
